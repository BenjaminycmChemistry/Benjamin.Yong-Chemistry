    <h2>Unit 1: States of Matter</h2>
     	<h2>1.1 Solids, Liquids and Gases</h2>
    
    <details>
        <summary>1. State the distinguishing properties of a solid in terms of its shape and volume.</summary>
        <div class="answer">A solid has a fixed shape and a fixed volume.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>2. How does the shape and volume of a liquid differ from a solid?</summary>
        <div class="answer">A liquid has a fixed volume but no fixed shape; it takes the shape of the container it is in.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>3. Describe the properties of a gas regarding shape and volume.</summary>
        <div class="answer">A gas has no fixed shape and no fixed volume.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>4. Describe the separation of particles in a solid compared to a gas.</summary>
        <div class="answer">Particles in a solid are very close together (touching), whereas in a gas, they are far apart.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>5. What is the arrangement of particles in a solid?</summary>
        <div class="answer">They are in a regular arrangement (or lattice).</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>6. Describe the arrangement of particles in a liquid.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They are in a random (or irregular) arrangement.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>7. What is the specific motion of particles in a solid?</summary>
        <div class="answer">They vibrate about fixed positions.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>8. Describe the motion of particles in a liquid.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They move around and slide over each other.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>9. Describe the motion of particles in a gas.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They move quickly and randomly in all directions.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>10. Define the change of state from solid to liquid and liquid to solid.</summary>
        <div class="answer">Solid to liquid is melting; liquid to solid is freezing.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>11. Define the changes of state from liquid to gas.</summary>
        <div class="answer">This can occur through boiling or evaporating.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>12. What is the name for the process of a gas turning into a liquid?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Condensing.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>13. What is the effect of increasing temperature on the volume of a gas?</summary>
        <div class="answer">It causes the volume to increase.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>14. What is the effect of increasing pressure on the volume of a gas?</summary>
        <div class="answer">It causes the volume to decrease.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>15. Explain melting in terms of kinetic particle theory.</summary>
        <div class="answer">As a solid is heated, particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate faster until they have enough energy to overcome the attractive forces holding them in a regular arrangement.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>16. Explain boiling in terms of kinetic particle theory.</summary>
        <div class="answer">As a liquid is heated, particles gain enough energy to completely overcome the forces of attraction between them, allowing them to move far apart as a gas.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>17. On a heating curve, what is happening to the temperature during a change of state?</summary>
        <div class="answer">The temperature remains constant (shown as a horizontal line) because the energy is being used to break the forces between particles.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>18. Explain, using kinetic particle theory, why increasing temperature increases gas volume.</summary>
        <div class="answer">Heating gives particles more kinetic energy, so they move faster and collide with the container walls more frequently and with more force, pushing them outward.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>19. Explain, using kinetic particle theory, why increasing pressure reduces gas volume.</summary>
        <div class="answer">Increased pressure forces the particles closer together, reducing the amount of empty space between them.</div>
    </details>

    <h2>1.2 Diffusion</h2>

    <details>
        <summary>20. What is diffusion?</summary>
        <div class="answer">The movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>21. Explain diffusion in terms of kinetic particle theory.</summary>
        <div class="answer">It occurs because particles are in constant random motion, which causes them to spread out and mix.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>22. Why does diffusion occur faster in gases than in liquids?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Gas particles have more kinetic energy and move much faster than liquid particles, with more space between them to move through.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>23. How does relative molecular mass affect the rate of diffusion of a gas?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Gases with a lower relative molecular mass diffuse faster than gases with a higher relative molecular mass.</div>
    </details>

    <details>
        <summary>24. Explain why a "lighter" gas diffuses faster than a "heavier" gas.</summary>
        <div class="answer">At any given temperature, particles with lower mass move faster on average than heavier particles.</div>
    </details>

<section id="u2" class="unit">
     <h2>2.1 Elements, Compounds and Mixtures</h2>
    <details>
        <summary>1. What is the definition of an element?</summary>
        <div class="answer">A substance that consists of only one type of atom.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>2. How is a compound defined in chemistry?</summary>
        <div class="answer">A substance formed from two or more different elements that are chemically combined.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>3. What is a mixture?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Two or more substances that are physically mixed together but not chemically combined.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>4. Describe a primary difference between a compound and a mixture regarding how they are joined.</summary>
        <div class="answer">In a compound, the elements are chemically bonded in fixed proportions, whereas in a mixture, the components are physically combined and can be separated by physical means.</div>
    </details>

    <h2>2.2 Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table</h2>
    <details>
        <summary>5. Describe the general structure of an atom.</summary>
        <div class="answer">An atom consists of a central nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons arranged in shells.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>6. What particles are found in the nucleus of an atom?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Protons and neutrons.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>7. Where are electrons located in an atom?</summary>
        <div class="answer">In shells surrounding the central nucleus.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>8. State the relative charge of a proton, a neutron, and an electron.</summary>
        <div class="answer">A proton has a charge of +1, a neutron has a charge of 0 (neutral), and an electron has a charge of -1.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>9. State the relative mass of a proton, a neutron, and an electron.</summary>
        <div class="answer">A proton has a mass of 1, a neutron has a mass of 1, and an electron has a mass that is negligible (approx. 1/1840).</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>10. Define the term "proton number" (also known as atomic number).</summary>
        <div class="answer">The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>11. Define the term "mass number" (also known as nucleon number).</summary>
        <div class="answer">The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>12. What is the electronic configuration of an atom with a proton number of 13?</summary>
        <div class="answer">2, 8, 3.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>13. What is unique about the electronic configuration of Group VIII noble gases?</summary>
        <div class="answer">They have a full outer electron shell.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>14. For elements in Groups I to VII, what is the relationship between the group number and the electronic configuration?</summary>
        <div class="answer">The number of outer shell electrons is equal to the group number.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>15. How is the period number of an element related to its electron shells?</summary>
        <div class="answer">The number of occupied electron shells is equal to the period number.</div>
    </details>

    <h2>2.3 Isotopes</h2>
    <details>
        <summary>16. Define "isotopes."</summary>
        <div class="answer">Different atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>17. In the symbol <sup>12</sup><sub>6</sub>C, what does the lower number (6) represent?</summary>
        <div class="answer">The proton number (atomic number).</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>18. In the symbol <sup>35</sup><sub>17</sub>Cl<sup>-</sup>, what does the negative superscript indicate?</summary>
        <div class="answer">It indicates that the species is an anion (a negative ion).</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>19. Why do isotopes of the same element have the same chemical properties?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Because they have the same number of electrons and therefore the same electronic configuration.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>20. How is the relative atomic mass of an element calculated?</summary>
        <div class="answer">It is calculated from the relative masses and the percentage abundances of all its isotopes.</div>
    </details>

    <h2>2.4 Ions and Ionic Bonds</h2>
    <details>
        <summary>21. How is a positive ion (cation) formed?</summary>
        <div class="answer">By an atom losing one or more electrons.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>22. How is a negative ion (anion) formed?</summary>
        <div class="answer">By an atom gaining one or more electrons.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>23. Define an "ionic bond."</summary>
        <div class="answer">A strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>24. Between which types of elements do ionic bonds typically form?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Between metallic and non-metallic elements.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>25. Describe the typical melting and boiling points of ionic compounds.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They have high melting points and high boiling points.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>26. Describe the electrical conductivity of ionic compounds in different states.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They have good electrical conductivity when aqueous or molten, but poor electrical conductivity when solid.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>27. Describe the "giant lattice structure" of ionic compounds.</summary>
        <div class="answer">A regular arrangement of alternating positive and negative ions.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>28. Explain why ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points in terms of their structure.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They have a giant lattice structure with strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions, which require significant thermal energy to overcome.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>29. Explain why ionic compounds conduct electricity when molten but not when solid.</summary>
        <div class="answer">In the solid state, ions are fixed in a lattice and cannot move; when molten or in solution, the ions are free to move and carry the electric charge.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>30. What diagram is used to show the formation of ionic bonds?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Dot-and-cross diagrams.</div>
    </details>

    <h2>2.5 Simple Molecules and Covalent Bonds</h2>
    <details>
        <summary>31. How is a covalent bond formed?</summary>
        <div class="answer">When a pair of electrons is shared between two atoms, leading to noble gas electronic configurations.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>32. Which simple molecules must you be able to describe using dot-and-cross diagrams?</summary>
        <div class="answer">H<sub>2</sub>, Cl<sub>2</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>O, CH<sub>4</sub>, NH<sub>3</sub>, HCl, CH<sub>3</sub>OH, C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>, O<sub>2</sub>, CO<sub>2</sub>, and N<sub>2</sub>.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>33. Describe the melting and boiling point characteristics of simple molecular compounds.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They have low melting points and boiling points.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>34. Describe the electrical conductivity of simple molecular compounds.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They have poor electrical conductivity.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>35. Explain the low melting and boiling points of simple molecular compounds in terms of bonding.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They have weak intermolecular forces between the molecules which require very little energy to overcome.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>36. Explain why simple molecular compounds are poor conductors of electricity.</summary>
        <div class="answer">They do not have delocalised electrons or mobile ions to carry a charge.</div>
    </details>

    <h2>2.6 Giant Covalent Structures</h2>
    <details>
        <summary>37. What are the two giant covalent structures of carbon described in the syllabus?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Graphite and diamond.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>38. Relate the structure of graphite to its use as a lubricant.</summary>
        <div class="answer">Graphite is arranged in layers that can slide over each other due to weak forces between them, making it slippery.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>39. Why is graphite used as an electrode?</summary>
        <div class="answer">It has delocalised electrons between its layers that allow it to conduct electricity.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>40. Why is diamond used in cutting tools?</summary>
        <div class="answer">It is extremely hard because every carbon atom is bonded to four others in a rigid giant covalent lattice.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>41. Describe the giant covalent structure of silicon(IV) oxide (SiO<sub>2</sub>).</summary>
        <div class="answer">It is a giant lattice where each silicon atom is covalently bonded to four oxygen atoms, and each oxygen atom is bonded to two silicon atoms.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>42. Why does silicon(IV) oxide share similar properties with diamond?</summary>
        <div class="answer">Both have giant covalent structures with strong covalent bonds throughout the entire lattice, resulting in high hardness and high melting points.</div>
    </details>

    <h2>2.7 Metallic Bonding</h2>
    <details>
        <summary>43. Define "metallic bonding."</summary>
        <div class="answer">The electrostatic attraction between positive ions in a giant metallic lattice and a ‘sea’ of delocalised electrons.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>44. Explain the good electrical conductivity of metals.</summary>
        <div class="answer">The delocalised electrons are free to move throughout the giant metallic lattice and carry an electric charge.</div>
    </details>
    <details>
        <summary>45. Explain why metals are malleable and ductile.</summary>
        <div class="answer">The layers of positive ions can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond because the delocalised electrons maintain the attraction.</div>
    </details>
